The Beginning

It started with one charpai and one daal.

In the summer of 1987, Bibi Kamran set up a single charpai under a peepal tree on the Grand Trunk Road, just outside Lahore. She cooked two things — daal makhani simmered overnight on a coal fire, and tandoori roti slapped fresh from a clay oven her son had built that spring.

Truck drivers stopped first. Then bus passengers. Then local families. By 1991, she had three charpais, a proper signboard, and a name people travelled for.

Today, that same daal is still simmered overnight, in copper pots her grandson still uses. We've grown — but the recipes, the spice blends, and the welcome haven't changed in forty years.

What We Believe

Four rules. We don't break them.

✦
01

Spices Ground Daily

Every masala you taste was whole spice this morning. We have a dedicated grinder room and a head spice cook.

02

No Microwave. Ever.

Curries are reheated in copper pots over low charcoal — the only way to keep their texture and aroma alive.

03

Fair Prices, Honest Portions

Our prices are set so a working family can eat here on a Friday night. Portions are dhaba-size, not "fine dining."

04

Everyone Eats the Same

The mutton in your karahi is the mutton in the staff lunch. The biryani we serve guests is the biryani we eat ourselves.

Our Journey

Forty Years on the Stove

✦
'87
One charpai under a peepal tree

Bibi Kamran sets up shop on GT Road outside Lahore. Two items on the menu: daal makhani and tandoori roti.

'94
The first proper kitchen

The dhaba moves into a small brick building. Bibi adds Friday paya and slow-cooked nihari.

'02
Second-generation chefs take over

Bibi's son Tariq takes the head chef seat, expanding the menu to forty-plus dishes — but never altering the originals.

'14
Featured in National Geographic

A roadside-food essay puts Kamran Dhaba on the global map. Lines start forming before sunrise on Friday halwa-puri days.

'21
Home delivery launches

For the first time, you can get our nihari hot at your door — packed in copper-lined containers to keep it traditional.

'26
Imran takes the apron

Bibi's grandson Imran becomes head chef — the third generation to oversee that same daal pot.

Chef's Note

"A dhaba isn't a restaurant. It's an open door."

My grandmother used to say that the moment a dhaba starts measuring its ghee in ounces instead of fistfuls, it stops being a dhaba. We still measure in fistfuls. We still keep the door open. And we still cook for whoever walks through it as if they were our own family.
